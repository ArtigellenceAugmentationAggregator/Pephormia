The FDA compounding hearing, explained without the hype

On 23-24 July 2026 the FDA reviews seven peptides for the 503A compounding list. Here is exactly what is on the table - and what it does not mean for you.

7 min read · 6 sources

On 23–24 July 2026, the FDA's Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee (PCAC) meets at the agency's White Oak campus to review seven peptides for the 503A Bulks List — the list of bulk substances that compounding pharmacies are allowed to make medicines from. It's docket FDA-2025-N-6895. This is an education-only summary of what's actually on the agenda, and, just as importantly, what it doesn't mean.

The seven peptides

Split across the two days, the committee is scheduled to evaluate:

  • Day one: BPC-157, KPV, TB-500, and MOTS-c.
  • Day two: DSIP (emideltide), Epitalon, and Semax.

Each is being assessed against specific proposed uses — wound healing, inflammatory conditions, insomnia, metabolic and body-composition claims, and so on.

What the committee is actually voting on

PCAC is an advisory committee. It votes on whether a substance is suitable to be added to the 503A Bulks List so that pharmacies can legally compound it. Right now, none of the seven meet the bar automatically: they have no USP monograph, no FDA-approved drug product, and no existing 503A listing.

An advisory vote is a recommendation, not a decision.

The FDA generally follows PCAC, but says it won't issue final determinations until after reviewing the committee's input.

What it doesn't mean

A favourable vote would let compounding pharmacies prepare these peptides under the 503A exemption. It would not make them FDA-approved drugs, would not confirm they work, and would not put them on a shelf as consumer products. An unfavourable vote points the other way — toward tighter restriction. Either way, the underlying evidence for most of these compounds remains early: strong mechanism in a dish, thin or absent controlled human data.

The Australian angle

None of this changes anything locally on its own. In Australia, most of these peptides are prescription or otherwise scheduled under the TGA's framework. A US compounding decision doesn't reclassify a substance here.

How we grade it
